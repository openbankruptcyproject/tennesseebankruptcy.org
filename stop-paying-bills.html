What Bills to Stop Paying Before Bankruptcy in Tennessee

Not all debts are created equal. Knowing which bills to stop paying -- and which to keep paying -- can save you thousands before you file.

Quick answer: Stop paying unsecured debts (credit cards, medical bills, personal loans) that will be discharged. Keep paying secured debts you want to keep (mortgage, car loan) and essential services (utilities, insurance). Do not make large payments to any single creditor in the 90 days before filing.

Bills You Can Stop Paying

  • Credit cards: All credit card debt is dischargeable. Stop paying, but stop using the cards too. New charges near filing can be challenged as fraud.
  • Medical bills: Medical debt is unsecured and fully dischargeable. Collections calls are annoying but temporary.
  • Personal loans: Unsecured personal loans from banks, online lenders, and finance companies are dischargeable.
  • Old utility bills: Past-due amounts on closed accounts are dischargeable. (Keep current utilities paid -- see below.)
  • Payday loans: Dischargeable unsecured debt. Payday lenders may threaten criminal action but cannot follow through on threats related to civil debt.
  • Collection accounts: Debts already in collections are dischargeable. The collection agency loses its right to collect once you file.

Bills You Should Keep Paying

  • Mortgage (if keeping the house): Falling behind creates arrears that complicate your case. In Chapter 13, you must be current on post-petition payments.
  • Car loan (if keeping the car): Same logic. Stay current on vehicles you need for work and daily life.
  • Current utilities: Electricity, water, gas, internet. Utility companies can require a deposit after bankruptcy but cannot shut off service solely because you filed.
  • Insurance: Auto insurance is legally required in Tennessee. Health insurance protects against new medical debt.
  • Child support/alimony: These are not dischargeable and must be paid. Falling behind creates a priority debt that must be paid in full in Chapter 13.
  • Taxes: Recent income taxes (within 3 years) are priority debts. Older tax debts may be dischargeable under specific conditions.

The 90-Day Preference Period

Under 11 U.S.C. section 547, the bankruptcy trustee can claw back payments made to creditors during the 90 days before filing if they exceed $600 to a single creditor. For payments to insiders (family, business partners), the look-back period is one year.

Common mistake: Paying off a credit card or repaying a family loan right before filing. The trustee can sue the recipient to recover that money for the benefit of all creditors. This is called a preferential transfer.
  • $600+ to one creditor in 90 days -- potentially recoverable
  • Any amount to family in 12 months -- potentially recoverable
  • Regular, ordinary payments (mortgage, car loan, utilities) -- generally safe

Frequently Asked Questions

What bills should I stop paying before bankruptcy in Tennessee?

Generally, you should continue paying secured debts you want to keep (mortgage, car loan) and essential bills (utilities, insurance). You can typically stop paying unsecured debts (credit cards, medical bills, personal loans) that will be discharged in bankruptcy. Consult with a bankruptcy attorney before making changes.

Should I stop paying credit cards before filing bankruptcy?

Yes, in most cases. Credit card debt is unsecured and will be discharged in bankruptcy. Continuing to pay credit cards before filing wastes money that could go toward filing costs or essential expenses. However, stop making new charges on the cards -- using credit cards with no intent to repay is fraud.

What is the preference period in bankruptcy?

The preference period is 90 days before filing (1 year for payments to insiders like family members). Payments of $600 or more to a single creditor during this period can be clawed back by the trustee as preferential transfers. Paying a favorite creditor right before filing can backfire.

Should I keep paying my mortgage before bankruptcy?

If you want to keep your house, yes -- keep paying the mortgage. If you plan to surrender the house, you can stop paying. In Chapter 13, mortgage arrears can be cured through the plan, but you must stay current on post-petition payments.

Should I keep paying my car loan before bankruptcy?

If you want to keep the vehicle, continue making payments. If you plan to surrender it, you can stop. In Chapter 13, you can cure arrears through the plan and may be able to reduce the loan balance to the vehicle's value (cramdown) if the loan is more than 910 days old.

Can creditors sue me if I stop paying before filing?

Yes, creditors can file lawsuits and obtain judgments. However, once you file bankruptcy, the automatic stay halts all collection activity. If you plan to file within 30-60 days, the risk of a completed lawsuit is low. A judgment lien, however, can complicate your bankruptcy.
