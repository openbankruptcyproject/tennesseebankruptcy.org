Bankruptcy and Employment in Tennessee

Federal law protects you from employment discrimination based on bankruptcy. Government employers have strict prohibitions. Private employer protections are narrower but still significant. Here is what Tennessee workers need to know.

Quick answer: Under 11 U.S.C. section 525(a), government employers cannot fire, refuse to hire, or discriminate against you based on bankruptcy. Under section 525(b), private employers cannot fire you solely for filing. However, most courts hold that private employers CAN consider bankruptcy in hiring decisions. Some states add extra protections.

Federal Protections: Section 525

Section 525(a): Government Employers

  • Cannot terminate based on bankruptcy
  • Cannot refuse to hire based on bankruptcy
  • Cannot discriminate in any way based on bankruptcy
  • Covers federal, state, and local government
  • Also covers government-issued licenses and permits
  • Broad and well-established protection

Section 525(b): Private Employers

  • Cannot terminate solely because of bankruptcy
  • Hiring: Most courts say section 525(b) does NOT prohibit refusing to hire
  • The word "hire" was deliberately omitted from 525(b)
  • Cannot discriminate against current employees
  • Narrower than government employer protections
  • Some states fill the gap with credit check restrictions

Tennessee-Specific Employment Protections

11 U.S.C. section 525(a) protects government employees. Tennessee does not have a state statute specifically prohibiting private employer bankruptcy discrimination. The Tennessee Human Rights Act (Tenn. Code 4-21) prohibits discrimination on certain grounds but does not list bankruptcy. The 6th Circuit (covering Tennessee) has addressed section 525(b). Tennessee's defense/energy sector (Oak Ridge, Arnold AFB, Fort Campbell) creates significant clearance-relevant employment.

Credit Checks in Employment

The Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) governs employer access to credit reports:

  • Written consent required: An employer must get your written permission before pulling your credit report
  • You can say no: But declining may affect your candidacy for positions requiring financial trust
  • Adverse action notice: If an employer takes adverse action based on your credit report, they must notify you and provide a copy of the report
  • Employment versions: Employers receive a modified credit report that does not include your credit score -- they see payment history, accounts, and public records (including bankruptcy)

Several states and cities restrict employer use of credit checks. Check whether Tennessee has additional restrictions.

Jobs Most Affected by Bankruptcy

While most jobs are unaffected by bankruptcy, certain positions may be more sensitive:

  • Financial industry: Banks, investment firms, insurance companies may consider bankruptcy for positions handling money
  • Government with clearance: Security clearance positions are affected but not disqualified (see our security clearance page)
  • Law enforcement: Some agencies consider financial history
  • Corporate officers: C-suite positions may have different standards
  • Bonded positions: Jobs requiring a surety bond may be affected if the bonding company considers bankruptcy

For the vast majority of jobs -- retail, healthcare, education, technology, manufacturing, service industry -- bankruptcy has no effect on employment.

Professional Licenses in Tennessee

Under section 525(a), Tennessee licensing authorities cannot deny, revoke, or refuse to renew professional licenses solely because of bankruptcy. This includes:

  • Real estate licenses
  • Medical and nursing licenses
  • Legal licenses (bar admission)
  • Teaching certificates
  • Trade licenses (plumbing, electrical, etc.)
  • Insurance agent licenses

If a licensing board requires a "financial responsibility" showing, the underlying financial condition (not the bankruptcy filing itself) is what matters. Successfully completing bankruptcy actually demonstrates financial responsibility.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I be fired for filing bankruptcy?

Government employers: absolutely not. Private employers: cannot fire you solely for bankruptcy under section 525(b). Using it as one factor among many is a gray area.

Can an employer refuse to hire me?

Government employers cannot. Most courts hold private employers can consider bankruptcy in hiring. Some states restrict credit check use in hiring, providing indirect protection.

Can my employer check my credit?

Only with your written consent under the FCRA. You can decline, but it may affect your candidacy for financial positions.

Does bankruptcy affect professional licenses?

Government licensing authorities cannot deny or revoke licenses based on bankruptcy under section 525(a). Financial responsibility requirements may still apply.

Should I tell my employer I filed?

Generally not required unless you are in a position with financial disclosure requirements. If wage garnishment stops because of bankruptcy, your employer may become aware. Focus on your job performance.
