Tax Refunds and Chapter 13 in Tennessee

In Chapter 13, the trustee may require you to turn over all or part of your tax refund as additional plan payments. Trustee practices vary by district, and there are strategies to protect some or all of your refund.

Quick answer: Most Chapter 13 trustees require debtors to turn over tax refunds above a threshold amount (typically $1,500-$2,500, but this varies by district). Your plan confirmation order or the trustee's standing instructions will specify requirements. Adjusting W-4 withholding to reduce the refund is one strategy, but must be done carefully to avoid bad-faith arguments.

Why the Trustee Wants Your Tax Refund

In Chapter 13, you commit all projected disposable income to your repayment plan for 3-5 years (11 U.S.C. section 1325(b)). The trustee's position is that a tax refund represents income that was over-withheld during the year -- money that should have been available to pay creditors.

The logic: if you receive a $4,000 refund, that is $333/month in excess withholding that was not included in your disposable income calculation. The trustee argues this should go to your creditors.

Tennessee Trustee Practices

Tennessee has no state income tax on wages (the Hall Tax was fully repealed in 2021). This means only federal tax refunds are at risk -- there is no state income tax refund to worry about. The Chapter 13 trustees in Tennessee's three districts are known to be aggressive about requiring federal tax refund turnover. The W.D. Tenn. (Memphis) and M.D. Tenn. (Nashville) in particular typically require refunds above $1,500-$2,000 to be turned over. Tennessee's wildcard exemption ($10,000 under Tenn. Code 26-2-103) can protect refund amounts.

Strategies to Protect Your Refund

1. Adjust Your W-4 Withholding

The most effective strategy is to adjust your W-4 so you get more per paycheck and a smaller refund. If your refund is $500, the trustee has little to claim. Do this gradually and early in the plan -- a sudden, dramatic change may trigger scrutiny.

2. Plan for the Refund in Your Budget

If you know the trustee will require turnover above $2,000, plan your withholding so your refund falls below that threshold. Use the IRS withholding estimator (irs.gov) to calibrate.

3. Request Permission to Keep the Refund

Some trustees allow debtors to keep refunds for specific purposes: necessary car repairs, medical expenses, or home maintenance. File a motion or request with the trustee explaining the need. Documentation helps (repair estimates, medical bills).

4. Earned Income Credit Arguments

If a significant portion of your refund is the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) or Child Tax Credit, some courts treat these as government benefits rather than disposable income. This argument has mixed results but is worth raising.

What Happens If You Do Not Turn Over the Refund

Failing to turn over a required tax refund can have serious consequences:

  • Motion to dismiss: The trustee can file a motion to dismiss your Chapter 13 case
  • Conversion to Chapter 7: The trustee or court may convert your case to Chapter 7
  • Bad faith finding: Repeated failure can be considered bad faith, affecting your ability to refile
  • Plan modification: The trustee may seek to modify your plan to increase payments
If you cannot turn over the refund or need to use it for an emergency, file a motion with the court BEFORE spending it. Courts are more sympathetic when you ask permission than when you ask forgiveness.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will the trustee take my tax refund?

Most likely some or all of it, depending on the amount and your district's practices. Check your plan terms for the specific threshold.

Can I adjust my withholding?

Yes, within reason. Gradual adjustments to reduce over-withholding are generally acceptable. Dramatic changes may be scrutinized.

What about Earned Income Credit?

Some courts treat EITC differently from regular refund amounts. This is not settled law everywhere -- results vary by district.

When must I turn over the refund?

Typically within 30-60 days of filing your return. Check the trustee's standing instructions or your plan confirmation order.

What if I spent the refund already?

Contact your attorney immediately. The trustee may file a motion to dismiss. Being proactive about communicating with the trustee is better than ignoring the requirement.
