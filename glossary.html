Tennessee Bankruptcy Glossary

Key bankruptcy terms and definitions for Tennessee filers, from automatic stay to wildcard exemption.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Bankruptcy Terms A-Z

341 Meeting (Meeting of Creditors)
A required hearing where the bankruptcy trustee (and any creditors who choose to attend) asks you questions under oath about your finances, assets, and petition. Held about 30 days after filing in W.D. Tenn., M.D. Tenn., and E.D. Tenn..
Adversary Proceeding
A lawsuit within a bankruptcy case, often filed to challenge the dischargeability of a specific debt or to recover fraudulent transfers.
Automatic Stay
A court order that takes effect immediately upon filing, stopping most collection actions, lawsuits, foreclosures, repossessions, and garnishments. See 11 U.S.C. section 362.
Chapter 7
Liquidation bankruptcy that eliminates most unsecured debt in 3-4 months. A trustee may sell non-exempt assets to pay creditors. Requires passing the means test.
Chapter 13
Reorganization bankruptcy for individuals with regular income. Debtor proposes a 3-5 year repayment plan to catch up on secured debts and pay a portion of unsecured debts.
Cramdown
Reducing the amount owed on a secured debt to the current value of the collateral. Available in Chapter 13 for vehicles purchased more than 910 days before filing and other secured debts.
Credit Counseling
A required course from an approved provider that must be completed within 180 days before filing bankruptcy. A second course (debtor education) is required before discharge.
Creditor
Any person or entity to whom you owe money. Includes secured creditors (backed by collateral), unsecured creditors (no collateral), and priority creditors (taxes, support obligations).
Discharge
A court order that permanently eliminates your personal liability for dischargeable debts. The discharge injunction (section 524) prevents creditors from collecting on discharged debts forever.
Disposable Income
Income remaining after subtracting allowed expenses. In Chapter 13, disposable income determines your plan payment amount.
Exemptions
Laws that protect specific property from being taken by the trustee in Chapter 7 or reduce what you pay in Chapter 13. Tennessee homestead exemption: $5,000 (individual) / $7,500 (joint); vehicle: $10,000.
Fraudulent Transfer
A transfer of property made to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors. The trustee can recover such transfers made within 2 years before filing (or longer under state law).
Lien
A legal claim on property as security for a debt. Common types include mortgage liens, auto liens, tax liens, and judgment liens.
Lien Stripping
Removing a wholly unsecured junior mortgage in Chapter 13. If the home value is less than the first mortgage balance, the second mortgage can be treated as unsecured debt.
Means Test
A formula under section 707(b) that determines Chapter 7 eligibility based on income and expenses. Compares your income to Tennessee median income for your household size.
Motion for Relief from Stay
A request by a creditor asking the court to lift the automatic stay, usually to allow foreclosure or repossession to proceed.
Non-Dischargeable Debt
Debts that survive bankruptcy, including student loans (absent undue hardship), most taxes, child support, alimony, debts from fraud, and DUI-related debts.
Petition
The official document filed with the bankruptcy court to start a case. Includes your personal information, chapter selected, and basic financial data.
Plan (Chapter 13)
A proposal showing how you will repay creditors over 3-5 years. Must pay priority debts in full, cure arrears on secured debts, and pay unsecured creditors at least what they would receive in Chapter 7.
Priority Debt
Debts that must be paid in full in Chapter 13 and cannot be discharged in Chapter 7. Includes recent taxes, child support, alimony, and certain government fines.
Proof of Claim
A form filed by a creditor establishing the amount and type of debt owed. Required for the creditor to receive payment in the case.
Reaffirmation Agreement
A voluntary agreement to remain liable for a debt after bankruptcy, typically used to keep a car or other secured property. Must be filed with the court.
Schedules
Detailed forms listing all assets, debts, income, expenses, and recent financial transactions. Required within 14 days of filing.
Secured Debt
A debt backed by collateral (like a mortgage or car loan). The creditor can repossess the collateral if the debt is not paid.
Trustee
A court-appointed official who administers your bankruptcy case. Chapter 7 trustees liquidate non-exempt assets. Chapter 13 trustees collect and distribute plan payments.
Unsecured Debt
A debt not backed by collateral, such as credit cards, medical bills, and personal loans. Generally dischargeable in bankruptcy.
Wildcard Exemption
An exemption that can be applied to any property type. Available under both state and federal exemption systems in Tennessee.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between Chapter 7 and Chapter 13?

Chapter 7 eliminates most unsecured debt in 3-4 months through liquidation of non-exempt assets. Chapter 13 is a 3-5 year repayment plan for people with regular income. Chapter 7 has income limits (means test); Chapter 13 has debt limits ($2,750,000).

What does discharge mean in bankruptcy?

A discharge is a permanent court order that eliminates your personal liability for specified debts. After discharge, creditors can never collect on those debts again. Not all debts are dischargeable -- student loans, most taxes, child support, and alimony typically survive.
