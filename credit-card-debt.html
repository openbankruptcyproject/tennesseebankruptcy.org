Credit Card Debt and Bankruptcy in Tennessee

Credit card debt is one of the most common reasons Tennessee residents file for bankruptcy. In most cases, credit card balances can be completely eliminated through Chapter 7 or significantly reduced through Chapter 13.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

How Each Chapter Handles Credit Card Debt

Chapter 7

Credit card debt is eliminated entirely in Chapter 7. The discharge typically comes in 3-4 months. You must pass the means test to qualify. Any non-exempt assets could be liquidated, but most Tennessee filers keep all their property under the state's exemptions.

Chapter 13

Credit card debt is included in your 3-5 year repayment plan. You pay a percentage of the total based on your disposable income. Many filers pay only 10-30 cents on the dollar. The remaining balance is discharged at the end of the plan.

Recent Charges: The 90-Day and 70-Day Rules

Under 11 U.S.C. section 523(a)(2)(C), certain recent credit card activity is presumed to be non-dischargeable:

  • Luxury goods/services over $800 charged within 90 days before filing
  • Cash advances over $1,100 within 70 days before filing

These are rebuttable presumptions. The credit card company must file an adversary proceeding to challenge discharge, and you can present evidence that the charges were made with the intent and ability to repay.

Best practice: Stop using credit cards well before filing. Using cards when you know you will file bankruptcy can be considered fraud.

Credit Card Lawsuits and Judgments in Tennessee

If a credit card company sues you in Tennessee and obtains a judgment, they can:

  • Garnish your wages
  • Levy your bank account
  • Place a lien on your property

Filing bankruptcy stops all of these through the automatic stay. Pre-existing judgment liens on personal property can often be avoided (removed) under 11 U.S.C. section 522(f) if they impair your Tennessee exemptions.

Tennessee Exemptions Relevant to Credit Card Debt

  • Homestead: $5,000 (individual) / $7,500 (joint) -- protects home equity from credit card judgment liens
  • Vehicle: $10,000
  • Federal exemptions: Available in Tennessee as an alternative to state exemptions

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I discharge credit card debt in Tennessee bankruptcy?

Yes. Credit card debt is unsecured debt and is generally dischargeable in both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 bankruptcy in Tennessee. In Chapter 7, it is eliminated entirely. In Chapter 13, you pay a percentage based on your disposable income over 3-5 years, and the rest is discharged.

Will recent credit card charges be discharged?

Charges of over $800 for luxury goods or services within 90 days of filing, and cash advances over $1,100 within 70 days of filing, are presumed non-dischargeable under 11 U.S.C. section 523(a)(2)(C). The creditor must object for this to apply.

Can creditors sue me for credit card debt?

Yes. In Tennessee, credit card companies can sue you, obtain a judgment, and garnish your wages or levy your bank account. Filing bankruptcy stops these actions through the automatic stay.
