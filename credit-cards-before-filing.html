Using Credit Cards Before Bankruptcy in Tennessee

Stop using credit cards once you decide to file bankruptcy. Charges made near filing can be challenged as fraud -- and the presumption works against you.

Quick answer: Do not use credit cards once you decide to file bankruptcy. Luxury purchases over $800 within 90 days and cash advances over $1,100 within 70 days are presumed fraudulent. These debts may survive your discharge.

The Two Presumption Periods

90-Day Rule: Luxury Goods

  • Applies to purchases of luxury goods/services
  • Threshold: $800+ to a single creditor
  • Within 90 days of filing
  • Presumed non-dischargeable (you must prove otherwise)
  • 11 U.S.C. section 523(a)(2)(C)(i)(I)

70-Day Rule: Cash Advances

  • Applies to cash advances
  • Threshold: $1,100+ in aggregate
  • Within 70 days of filing
  • Presumed non-dischargeable (you must prove otherwise)
  • 11 U.S.C. section 523(a)(2)(C)(i)(II)

What You Should Do

Stop Using Cards Immediately

The moment you decide to file bankruptcy, stop all credit card use. Here is why:

  • Every charge creates risk. Even necessary purchases can be challenged if made close to filing.
  • Intent matters. Using a credit card when you know you will not repay it is the definition of fraud in this context.
  • The burden shifts to you. Within the 90/70 day windows, YOU must prove the charges were not fraudulent. Outside those windows, the creditor must prove fraud.

What Charges Are Safe?

  • Groceries and essential food -- not luxury, reasonably necessary
  • Gas for work commute -- necessary for income
  • Medical expenses -- necessary for health
  • Basic utilities -- if auto-billed to the card, switch to bank payment
Even "safe" charges should be avoided if possible. Use cash or debit instead. The cleaner your pre-filing credit card activity, the smoother your case.

What Happens If Charges Are Challenged

If a credit card company believes you ran up charges with no intent to repay, they can file an adversary proceeding -- a lawsuit within your bankruptcy case. The process:

  1. Creditor files a complaint alleging non-dischargeability under section 523(a)(2)
  2. You must respond within 30 days
  3. Discovery phase: both sides exchange evidence (statements, purchase records)
  4. Trial or settlement: most adversary proceedings settle before trial
  5. If the creditor wins, those specific charges survive your discharge

Adversary proceedings are expensive and time-consuming for both sides. Creditors typically only file them for significant amounts ($2,000+). But avoiding the risk entirely is the smartest strategy.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use credit cards before filing bankruptcy in Tennessee?

Technically you can, but it is strongly discouraged. Charges made with no intent to repay can be challenged as fraud. Luxury purchases over $800 within 90 days of filing are presumed fraudulent. Cash advances over $1,100 within 70 days are also presumed fraudulent.

What is the 90-day rule for credit cards before bankruptcy?

Under 11 U.S.C. section 523(a)(2)(C), charges for luxury goods or services exceeding $800 made within 90 days of filing bankruptcy are presumed to be non-dischargeable. The creditor does not have to prove fraud -- you must prove the charges were not fraudulent.

What is the 70-day rule for cash advances?

Cash advances exceeding $1,100 taken within 70 days of filing are presumed non-dischargeable under 11 U.S.C. section 523(a)(2)(C). This includes balance transfers, convenience checks, and ATM cash advances.

What counts as a luxury purchase?

Luxury goods and services are items not reasonably necessary for support or maintenance. Groceries, gas, medical expenses, and basic clothing are not luxury. Electronics, jewelry, vacations, expensive dining, and entertainment are typically considered luxury.

What happens if I used credit cards right before filing?

The credit card company may file an adversary proceeding arguing the charges are non-dischargeable due to fraud. If the charges are within the 90/70 day presumption periods, you bear the burden of proving they were not fraudulent. Charges outside those windows require the creditor to prove actual fraud.

Should I close my credit cards before filing bankruptcy?

Do not close accounts before filing -- it does not help and can look like you are trying to hide something. Simply stop using the cards. They will be listed in your bankruptcy schedules and the issuers will close them after receiving notice of your filing.
