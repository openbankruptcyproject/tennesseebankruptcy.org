Retirement Accounts and Bankruptcy in Tennessee

Your retirement savings are among the most protected assets in bankruptcy. ERISA-qualified plans are 100% exempt under federal law, and IRAs are protected up to $1,512,350. Here is what Tennessee filers need to know.

Quick answer: Your 401(k), 403(b), pension, and other ERISA-qualified retirement plans are 100% exempt in bankruptcy -- no dollar limit. IRAs (traditional and Roth) are protected up to $1,512,350. Do NOT cash out retirement to pay debts or avoid filing. That converts protected money into unprotected money.

ERISA Plans: 100% Protected

Under federal law (11 U.S.C. section 522(b)(3)(C) and ERISA section 206(d)), employer-sponsored retirement plans are completely exempt from the bankruptcy estate. This includes:

  • 401(k) plans -- no dollar limit on exemption
  • 403(b) plans (tax-sheltered annuities) -- fully exempt
  • Defined benefit pension plans -- fully exempt
  • Profit-sharing plans -- fully exempt
  • 457(b) government plans -- fully exempt
  • Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) -- fully exempt for federal employees

This protection applies nationwide regardless of state exemption choices. The trustee cannot touch these accounts.

IRAs: Protected Up to $1,512,350

Traditional IRAs and Roth IRAs are protected under 11 U.S.C. section 522(n) up to a combined limit of $1,512,350 (adjusted for inflation). This limit applies to:

  • Traditional IRAs
  • Roth IRAs
  • SEP-IRAs (may receive full ERISA protection if employer-sponsored)
  • SIMPLE IRAs (may receive full ERISA protection)

Rollover amounts from an ERISA plan into an IRA retain their unlimited ERISA protection -- they do not count against the $1,512,350 cap.

Tennessee-Specific Retirement Protections

Tennessee does not allow federal exemptions. ERISA-qualified plans are 100% exempt under federal law. Tennessee exempts retirement accounts under Tenn. Code 26-2-105: IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s, and other qualified plans are exempt. Tennessee Consolidated Retirement System (TCRS) benefits are fully exempt. Tennessee has no state income tax on wages (the Hall Tax on investment income was fully repealed in 2021), which simplifies the retirement income picture.

Important: Tennessee uses state only (no federal option) exemptions. If you have a choice between state and federal exemptions, compare the total exemption picture -- retirement is just one factor.

The Biggest Mistake: Cashing Out Retirement

Every year, thousands of people cash out retirement accounts to pay debts or avoid bankruptcy. This is almost always a mistake because:

  • You lose the exemption. Money in a retirement account is protected. Money in a bank account is not (or minimally protected).
  • You owe income taxes on the entire withdrawal amount.
  • You pay a 10% early withdrawal penalty if under age 59 1/2.
  • The debts may be dischargeable anyway. Credit card debt, medical debt, and personal loans are wiped out in bankruptcy -- you could have kept the retirement money and discharged the debt.
  • You cannot replace the money. Annual contribution limits mean it takes years to rebuild what you withdrew.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep my 401(k) in Tennessee bankruptcy?

Yes. ERISA-qualified plans are 100% exempt under federal law. Your 401(k) is completely protected regardless of balance.

Are IRAs protected?

Yes, up to $1,512,350 combined for all traditional and Roth IRAs. Rollover amounts from ERISA plans keep their unlimited protection.

Can I contribute to retirement during Chapter 13?

Generally yes at your existing level. Increasing contributions during the plan may be challenged by the trustee as reducing disposable income.

What about inherited IRAs?

Inherited IRAs are NOT protected in bankruptcy (Clark v. Rameker, 2014). Only IRAs you funded yourself are exempt. Spousal inherited IRAs may be protected if rolled into your own IRA.

Should I cash out retirement to avoid bankruptcy?

Almost never. You lose the exemption, owe taxes, pay penalties, and the debt might have been dischargeable. Talk to a bankruptcy attorney before touching retirement accounts.

Can creditors garnish retirement accounts?

ERISA plans are generally protected from garnishment. Exceptions: IRS tax levies, divorce QDROs, and certain federal criminal penalties.
