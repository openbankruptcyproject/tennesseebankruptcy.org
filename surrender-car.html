Surrendering Your Car in Tennessee Bankruptcy

Surrendering a vehicle in bankruptcy eliminates both the car payment and any deficiency balance. This can be the right choice when you owe more than the car is worth or cannot afford the payments.

Quick answer: Surrender means returning the vehicle to the lender. In Chapter 7, the deficiency balance (what you owe minus what the car sells for) is discharged -- you owe nothing. In Chapter 13, the deficiency becomes unsecured debt in your plan. Surrender is often the smartest move for upside-down loans.

How Car Surrender Works

Step-by-Step Process

  1. File bankruptcy and indicate "surrender" on your Statement of Intention (Chapter 7) or plan (Chapter 13)
  2. Automatic stay activates -- the lender cannot repossess while the stay is in effect
  3. Return the vehicle -- the lender arranges pickup or designates a drop-off location (usually 30-45 days after filing)
  4. Lender sells the vehicle -- typically at auction, often for wholesale value
  5. Deficiency balance calculated -- what you owe minus sale price
  6. Deficiency discharged -- in Chapter 7, the deficiency is eliminated; in Chapter 13, it becomes unsecured debt in your plan

Tennessee Vehicle Exemptions

Tennessee's wildcard exemption ($10,000) can be applied to vehicle equity, which is relatively generous. Surrendering an underwater vehicle eliminates the deficiency in Chapter 7. Tennessee's three major metros (Nashville, Memphis, Knoxville) have limited public transit, making personal transportation important. The deficiency balance is discharged.

When Surrender Makes Sense

Consider Surrender When

  • You owe significantly more than the car is worth (upside down)
  • The payment is unaffordable
  • The vehicle has high mileage or needs expensive repairs
  • You have access to alternative transportation
  • You can acquire a cheaper replacement vehicle
  • Interest rate is very high (20%+ on a buy-here-pay-here loan)

Consider Keeping When

  • You have positive equity (worth more than you owe)
  • The payment is affordable on your budget
  • The vehicle is reliable and you need it for work
  • You are close to paying it off
  • Chapter 13 cramdown can reduce the balance to current value
  • Interest rate is reasonable

The Chapter 13 Cramdown Alternative

If your car loan is more than 910 days old (approximately 2.5 years), Chapter 13 offers a powerful alternative to surrender: the cramdown. Under 11 U.S.C. section 1325(a), you can reduce the secured claim to the vehicle's current value and pay that amount through your plan at a court-approved interest rate.

Example: You owe $15,000 on a car worth $8,000. If the loan is older than 910 days, you pay $8,000 as a secured claim in your plan. The remaining $7,000 becomes unsecured debt (paid at whatever percentage your plan pays unsecured creditors -- often pennies on the dollar).

The 910-day rule (sometimes called the "hanging paragraph") prevents cramdown on newer vehicle purchases.

Getting a Replacement Vehicle

After surrender, you will need replacement transportation. Options include:

  • Buy-here-pay-here lots: Available immediately but often have high interest rates and limited selection
  • Cash purchase: Save up for a reliable used car ($3,000-$5,000 can get a dependable vehicle)
  • Credit union auto loans: Some credit unions will lend to recent bankruptcy filers, especially members
  • Subprime auto lenders: Available after discharge, interest rates will be high but can be refinanced later as credit improves
  • Family or friends: A temporary arrangement while you rebuild

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens when I surrender my car?

You return it, the lender sells it, and the deficiency is discharged in Chapter 7 or becomes unsecured debt in Chapter 13.

Do I have to surrender?

No. You can reaffirm, redeem, or (in Chapter 13) cramdown. Surrender is one option among several.

When do I give up the car?

Typically 30-45 days after filing. The lender arranges pickup or a drop-off. You can drive until then.

Will I owe after surrender?

In Chapter 7, no -- the deficiency is discharged. In Chapter 13, it becomes unsecured debt in your plan.

Can I surrender a leased car?

Yes. You can reject the lease in bankruptcy. Early termination fees and remaining lease obligations are dischargeable.
