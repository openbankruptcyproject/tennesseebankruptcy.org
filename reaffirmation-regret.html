Undoing a Reaffirmation Agreement in Tennessee

Reaffirmation agreements make you personally liable for a debt that would otherwise be discharged. If you regret signing one, you may have options - especially if you are still within the 60-day rescission window.

Quick answer: You can rescind (cancel) a reaffirmation agreement within 60 days of filing it with the court, or before discharge, whichever is later (11 U.S.C. section 524(c)(4)). No reason needed - just send written notice to the creditor. After 60 days, options are limited but include negotiation, voluntary surrender, or a new filing.

The 60-Day Rescission Window

Under 11 U.S.C. section 524(c)(4), you have an absolute right to rescind any reaffirmation agreement at any time before discharge or within 60 days after the agreement is filed with the court, whichever is later.

  • No reason required - you do not have to explain why
  • Written notice to the creditor is sufficient
  • The debt becomes dischargeable again once rescinded
  • The creditor may repossess the collateral (vehicle, etc.) after rescission
If you are considering rescission, act quickly. Count the days carefully from when the agreement was filed with the court (check your case docket), not when you signed it.

Options After the 60-Day Window

If You Cannot Afford the Payments

After the rescission window closes, you have several options:

  • Negotiate with the creditor: Request lower payments, reduced interest, or loan modification. Creditors often prefer this to repossession/foreclosure.
  • Refinance: If your credit has improved since filing, refinancing at a lower rate may make payments affordable.
  • Voluntary surrender: Return the property. You will owe any deficiency balance (the difference between what you owe and what the property sells for). This is a regular debt - not discharged because you reaffirmed it.
  • File a new bankruptcy: If eligible under 11 U.S.C. section 727(a)(8) (Chapter 7: 8 years between filings) or section 1328(f) (Chapter 13: varies), the reaffirmed debt can be discharged in the new case.

Tennessee-Specific Considerations

Tennessee uses state exemptions only. Tennessee's motor vehicle exemption is quite limited. The E.D. Tenn., M.D. Tenn., and W.D. Tenn. courts all review reaffirmation agreements. The 60-day rescission period applies. Tennessee Consumer Protection Act (Tenn. Code 47-18) provides additional remedies against deceptive practices.

Common Reaffirmation Regrets

Vehicle Reaffirmation

The most common reaffirmation regret involves vehicles. Debtors reaffirm a car loan to keep the vehicle but later realize the payment is unsustainable or the car is worth far less than the loan balance. Without reaffirmation, the debt would have been discharged - you could have surrendered the car and owed nothing.

Mortgage Reaffirmation

Most attorneys advise against mortgage reaffirmation. Without reaffirmation, you can keep making payments and stay in the home. If you ever need to walk away, the deficiency is discharged. With reaffirmation, you are personally liable for any deficiency after foreclosure.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I undo a reaffirmation agreement?

Within 60 days of filing or before discharge: yes, automatically. After that: options are limited to negotiation, refinancing, surrender, or a new bankruptcy filing.

What happens after the 60-day window?

The agreement is binding. You can negotiate with the creditor, refinance, voluntarily surrender, or file a new bankruptcy if eligible.

Do I need a reason to rescind?

No. The right to rescind within 60 days is absolute - no reason or justification needed. Simply send written notice to the creditor.

What if the court approved my reaffirmation?

Court approval does not prevent rescission during the 60-day window. The right to rescind under section 524(c)(4) applies regardless of court approval.

Should I have reaffirmed in the first place?

That depends on the specific asset and your financial situation. Many attorneys advise against reaffirmation (especially on mortgages) because it restores personal liability on debt that could have been discharged.
