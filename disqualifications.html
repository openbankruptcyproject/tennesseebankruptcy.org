Bankruptcy Disqualifications in Tennessee

Not everyone qualifies for bankruptcy relief. Understanding the disqualifications can help Tennessee residents plan their filing strategy.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Grounds for Denial of Discharge

Under 11 U.S.C. section 727(a), the court can deny your Chapter 7 discharge entirely if:

  • Concealment or transfer of property with intent to defraud within 1 year before filing or during the case (section 727(a)(2))
  • Destruction or concealment of records relating to your financial condition (section 727(a)(3))
  • Perjury or false statements in connection with the case (section 727(a)(4))
  • Failure to explain loss of assets or deficiency of assets (section 727(a)(5))
  • Failure to complete the post-filing financial management course (section 727(a)(11))
  • Prior discharge within the applicable time period (section 727(a)(8) and (a)(9))

Credit Counseling Requirement

You cannot file bankruptcy in W.D. Tenn., M.D. Tenn., and E.D. Tenn. without first completing a credit counseling course from an approved provider within 180 days before filing. There is no exception except:

  • Exigent circumstances that merit a waiver (very rarely granted)
  • The approved providers for your district are unable to provide services (almost never happens)

Courses can be completed online, by phone, or in person and typically take about one hour. A second course (debtor education / financial management) is required after filing but before discharge.

180-Day Dismissal Bar

Under 11 U.S.C. section 109(g), you cannot file a new bankruptcy petition if a prior case was dismissed within the past 180 days for:

  • Willful failure to obey orders of the court
  • Willful failure to appear before the court
  • Filing a motion to dismiss after a creditor requested relief from the automatic stay
If you have had a recent dismissal, count the days carefully. Filing before the 180-day period expires can result in immediate dismissal of the new case.

Means Test Disqualification (Chapter 7 Only)

If your income is above the Tennessee median and you do not pass the means test expense deductions, you cannot file Chapter 7. Your options are:

  • File Chapter 13 instead (no income ceiling)
  • Wait until your income drops (e.g., job change, retirement)
  • Recalculate after 6 months if a high-income period falls out of the lookback window

Frequently Asked Questions

What disqualifies you from filing bankruptcy in Tennessee?

You may be disqualified from bankruptcy if: you did not complete credit counseling, you received a recent prior discharge (8 years for Ch.7-to-Ch.7, 4 years for Ch.7-to-Ch.13, 6 years for Ch.13-to-Ch.7, 2 years for Ch.13-to-Ch.13), you committed fraud, your prior case was dismissed within 180 days for cause, or your income is too high for Chapter 7 and debts exceed Chapter 13 limits.

Can I be denied a bankruptcy discharge?

Yes. Even if you are allowed to file, the court can deny discharge for cause under section 727(a). Grounds include concealing assets, destroying records, committing perjury, failing to explain loss of assets, or failing to complete the financial management course.

What happens if my bankruptcy was recently dismissed in Tennessee?

If a case was dismissed within the past 180 days for willful failure to obey a court order, appear before the court, or properly prosecute the case, you cannot file a new case immediately. Additionally, repeat filings within one year can limit or eliminate automatic stay protection.
