Can You Keep Your Car in Nashville Bankruptcy?

Most Nashville residents can keep their car in bankruptcy. Here is how.

Options for Cars with Loans

  • Reaffirmation: Keep paying, keep the car

Keeping Your Car in a Nashville Bankruptcy

For Nashville filers, the car is often the second most important asset to protect after the house. Your car is how you get to work, how you get to the bankruptcy court, and - if you lose it - often how you spiral back into financial trouble. The good news: most filers keep their vehicles. The Bankruptcy Code offers three separate tools (reaffirmation, redemption, and cramdown) and the state motor vehicle exemption protects the equity in paid-off cars. Here is how to use each one.

Reaffirmation (Chapter 7). If you are current on your car loan and want to keep paying, you sign a reaffirmation agreement with the lender. Reaffirmation removes the car loan from the discharge, meaning you remain personally liable going forward. The judge has to approve reaffirmation for represented debtors and must find that it does not impose an undue hardship. Most lenders require reaffirmation to continue reporting on-time payments to credit bureaus, so reaffirming can help rebuild credit - but if you default later, the lender can sue you for a deficiency after repossession. Think carefully before reaffirming on a car you are upside-down on.

Redemption (Chapter 7). Under 11 U.S.C. section 722, you can redeem a car by paying the lender the car's current fair market value in a single lump sum. If you owe $15,000 on a car worth $8,000, you can wipe out the lien by paying the lender $8,000. The catch is finding the cash - many filers use a redemption lender like 722 Redemption Funding. Redemption is powerful for underwater cars but not available if you are current and want to stay that way.

Cramdown (Chapter 13). If you bought the car more than 910 days before filing and it is worth less than you owe, Chapter 13 lets you "cram down" the loan to the car's current value. The secured portion is paid at the car's value over the plan term with a new interest rate set by the court (usually prime plus a risk premium). The deficiency - the difference between the original loan balance and the new secured amount - is treated as unsecured and usually paid pennies on the dollar. The 910-day rule cuts off cramdown for newer purchase-money car loans, but any refinance, any non-purchase-money lien, or any vehicle purchased more than 910 days ago is fair game.

Paid-Off Cars and the Exemption

If your car is paid off, the question is whether your equity fits within the Tennessee motor vehicle exemption (and any unused wildcard exemption you can stack on top). State exemption amounts typically run from $2,000 to $10,000 per vehicle. If the car is worth more than your exemption, a Chapter 7 trustee could theoretically sell it - but in practice, trustees rarely bother unless the excess equity is substantial. In Chapter 13, you keep the car and pay the non-exempt value to unsecured creditors through the plan.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens to my car payment during the bankruptcy case?

You keep paying it. The automatic stay stops repossession, but it does not excuse you from making your monthly payment. If you want to keep the car, stay current. Some trustees allow you to skip one payment at filing if you are rolling it into a Chapter 13 plan, but the default assumption is you keep paying the car lender directly.

Can I keep my car in a Nashville Chapter 7 if I am behind on payments?

Generally no, unless you can catch up before the reaffirmation deadline. Chapter 7 does not have a cure mechanism for car loan arrears the way it does for mortgages. If you are behind, Chapter 13 is the answer - Chapter 13 lets you add the arrearage to your plan payment and cure the default over 36 to 60 months while keeping the car.

Is cramdown available on a car I bought last year?

No. The 'hanging paragraph' at the end of 11 U.S.C. section 1325(a) bars cramdown for purchase-money security interests in vehicles acquired within 910 days before filing, where the vehicle is for personal use. Non-purchase-money loans (refinances, private lenders) and vehicles purchased for business use or owned longer than 910 days remain eligible for cramdown.
