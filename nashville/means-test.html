Walking Through the Nashville Means Test Step by Step

The bankruptcy means test is the federal gatekeeper between you and Chapter 7 relief. For Nashville residents, it has two stages, and most filers pass at the first stage without ever needing the second. The test compares your last six months of gross household income (multiplied by two for an annualized figure) against the Tennessee state median shown above. If you are under the median, you automatically qualify for Chapter 7 and the rest of the test does not apply.

If your annualized income is above the Tennessee median, the means test moves to a second calculation. You subtract a set of allowed monthly expenses - IRS National Standards for food, clothing, and household supplies; Local Standards for housing and transportation set specifically for Davidson County; your actual health insurance, childcare, and court-ordered support payments; and secured debt payments on your house and car. What is left is your "monthly disposable income." If that number is low enough, you still pass.

A few Nashville-specific notes: the Middle District of Tennessee uses the IRS Local Standards for Davidson and surrounding counties, which tend to be higher than rural Tennessee counties. That can help borderline filers. Social Security retirement and disability income is excluded entirely from the income calculation. VA disability is also excluded. If you are a disabled veteran whose Chapter 7 debts are primarily from active duty, you can skip the means test altogether under 11 U.S.C. section 707(b)(2)(D).

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Counting only your income. The test looks at household income, including a non-filing spouse, with certain marital deductions available.
  • Using gross pay from a pay stub. Use actual deposits over the six months preceding the month you file. Overtime, bonuses, and commissions all count.
  • Forgetting tax refunds. Refunds are not "income" for the means test, but unspent refunds sitting in your account on the filing date are assets the trustee can reach.
  • Filing the wrong month. A single high-income month rolling off your six-month window can drop you below median. Timing matters.

If the means test is the only thing standing between you and discharge, a Nashville Chapter 13 case is almost always available as a fallback - Chapter 13 has no means test at the front door, only a disposable income calculation for the plan payment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the Nashville means test count my spouse's income if I file alone?

Yes. Household income is combined for the means test regardless of who is filing. However, a non-filing spouse's income spent solely on their own separate obligations - their own car loan, their separate credit card, their student loans - can be deducted as a 'marital adjustment' on Form 122A-1 Line 13.

What if my income just went down but the six-month lookback still shows me above median?

You have two choices. First, wait to file until enough low-income months roll into the lookback window to drop you below median. Second, file now under Chapter 13 and convert to Chapter 7 later once your six-month average drops. A third option - arguing 'special circumstances' under section 707(b)(2)(B) - is available but requires documented unique circumstances like a serious medical condition or active-duty call-up.

Are VA disability payments counted in the Nashville means test?

No. VA disability, Social Security retirement, and Social Security disability (SSDI) are all excluded from current monthly income for means test purposes. If you are a disabled veteran whose debts are primarily incurred during active duty or while performing a homeland defense activity, you may be fully exempt from the means test under 11 U.S.C. section 707(b)(2)(D).
