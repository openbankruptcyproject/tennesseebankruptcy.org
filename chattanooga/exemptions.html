Tennessee Exemptions Explained for Chattanooga Filers

Exemptions are the rules that decide what you keep when you file bankruptcy. Everything you own becomes part of the bankruptcy estate the moment you file, and exemptions are the legal carve-outs that pull property back out of the estate and into your hands. For Chattanooga residents, the exemption question has three parts: which set of exemptions you can use, how much each exemption protects, and what happens to property that exceeds the limits.

State exemption law governs in most Tennessee bankruptcy cases. The federal exemptions in 11 U.S.C. section 522(d) are available only in "opt-in" states; in opt-out states, filers must use the state set. Check your state's current opt-in status before relying on federal numbers - some states allow a choice, others do not. In either case, you cannot mix and match: you pick one entire set of exemptions, not the best items from each.

The biggest exemption for most filers is the homestead - the equity in your primary residence you can protect from creditors. Homestead amounts vary wildly by state, from a few thousand dollars in some states to unlimited in others (Florida, Texas, Kansas, Iowa, Oklahoma, South Dakota). Domicile rules matter here: if you moved to your current state within the last 730 days, you may have to use the exemptions of your previous state under 11 U.S.C. section 522(b)(3)(A). Plan the filing timing accordingly.

Common Exemption Categories

  • Homestead: Equity in your primary residence.
  • Motor vehicle: Equity in one or more vehicles, usually a few thousand dollars per vehicle.
  • Personal property: Household goods, clothing, appliances, up to a cap.
  • Tools of the trade: Equipment you need to earn a living.
  • Retirement accounts: 401(k), pension, and IRA balances are almost always fully protected.
  • Wildcard: A dollar amount you can apply to any property, including cash.
  • Life insurance cash value: Usually protected up to a cap.
  • Public benefits: Social Security, unemployment, workers compensation, veterans benefits.

If you own property that exceeds your exemption limit - say, a paid-off car worth $12,000 when your exemption protects only $4,000 - the Chapter 7 trustee has the right to sell it, pay you the exempt amount, and distribute the rest to your creditors. In practice, trustees often negotiate with the debtor to buy the non-exempt portion rather than actually liquidate. Alternatively, you can file Chapter 13 and pay the non-exempt value to unsecureds through your plan instead of surrendering the asset.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use the federal bankruptcy exemptions instead of Tennessee?

It depends on whether Tennessee has opted out of the federal exemption scheme. Most states have opted out, meaning you must use the state exemptions. A handful of states (and DC) allow filers to choose. Confirm current opt-in status before you file - it changes how you fill out Schedule C.

What if I moved to this state less than two years ago?

Under 11 U.S.C. section 522(b)(3)(A), if you have not been domiciled in your current state for the 730 days before filing, you use the exemptions of the state where you lived the greater part of the 180 days before that 730-day window. If that earlier state only allows its exemptions for residents, you may be forced into the federal set as a fallback.

Are retirement accounts safe in bankruptcy?

Yes, almost always. ERISA-qualified plans (401(k), 403(b), pension) are excluded from the estate entirely. Traditional and Roth IRAs are exempt up to a federal cap that is adjusted periodically - currently over $1.5 million in aggregate. Inherited IRAs are treated differently and may not receive the same protection under Clark v. Rameker (2014).
