Filing Bankruptcy for Medical Debt in Tennessee

Medical debt is the leading cause of bankruptcy in America. It is fully dischargeable in both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13. You do not need to feel guilty about discharging medical debt -- the system is designed for exactly this situation.

Quick answer: Medical debt is 100% dischargeable in bankruptcy. You cannot file only for medical debt (all eligible debts are included), but medical bills are unsecured, non-priority debt -- the easiest category to discharge. Before filing, check if you qualify for hospital charity care or financial assistance programs in Tennessee.

Medical Debt in Bankruptcy: The Basics

Medical debt is classified as general unsecured, non-priority debt in bankruptcy -- the same category as credit cards and personal loans. This is the most favorable classification for discharge:

  • Chapter 7: Medical debt is completely eliminated when you receive your discharge (typically 3-4 months after filing)
  • Chapter 13: Medical debt is paid at whatever percentage your plan pays to unsecured creditors. In many Chapter 13 plans, unsecured creditors receive 10% or less. A $50,000 medical bill might result in paying $5,000 or less over 3-5 years.
  • No exception to discharge: Unlike student loans or certain tax debts, there is no exception that prevents medical debt from being discharged

Tennessee Medical Debt Protections

Medical debt is fully dischargeable in Tennessee bankruptcy. Tennessee did not expand Medicaid under the ACA for most of the period (a block-grant waiver was recently approved), leading to a significant coverage gap that increased medical debt. Tennessee does not have specific medical debt protection statutes. Tennessee hospitals receiving Hill-Burton funds must provide charity care. Medical debt is a leading driver of bankruptcy in Tennessee.

Before Filing: Exhaust Other Options

Hospital Financial Assistance (Charity Care)

Under the ACA, nonprofit hospitals are required to have financial assistance policies. Many hospitals will significantly reduce or eliminate bills for patients below certain income thresholds (typically 200-400% of the federal poverty level). Ask the billing department about financial assistance before paying or filing bankruptcy.

Negotiate the Bill

Medical bills are often negotiable. Strategies include:

  • Request an itemized bill and dispute any errors
  • Ask for the "self-pay" or "uninsured" rate (often 40-60% less than the billed amount)
  • Offer a lump-sum settlement for less than the full amount
  • Request a payment plan with no interest

When Bankruptcy Makes More Sense

Bankruptcy is often the better choice when:

  • Medical debt is combined with other unsecured debts (credit cards, personal loans)
  • Total debt exceeds your ability to repay within a reasonable time
  • Creditors are suing or garnishing wages
  • The hospital denies charity care or the remaining balance is still unaffordable
  • Medical debt is on top of existing financial stress

Credit Report Changes for Medical Debt

Recent changes to credit reporting have reduced the impact of medical debt:

  • Medical debt under $500: No longer reported by Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion (as of 2023)
  • Paid medical collections: Removed from credit reports
  • 1-year waiting period: Medical debt cannot appear on credit reports until at least 1 year after going to collections
  • FICO 10T and VantageScore 4.0: Newer scoring models reduce the weight of medical collections

Despite these improvements, large medical debts sent to third-party collectors can still damage your credit. Bankruptcy provides a definitive resolution.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I file bankruptcy just for medical debt?

You cannot selectively discharge only medical debt -- all eligible unsecured debts are included. But if medical debt is your primary problem, bankruptcy eliminates it along with other unsecured debts.

Is medical debt dischargeable?

Yes, 100%. Medical debt is unsecured, non-priority debt -- the easiest category to discharge. There is no exception preventing discharge.

Should I negotiate or file bankruptcy?

If medical debt is your only debt, try negotiation and charity care first. If you have multiple debts or the amounts are unmanageable, bankruptcy may be more efficient.

Will medical debt affect my credit?

Medical debt under $500 is no longer reported. Paid medical collections are removed. Large unpaid amounts can still affect credit until resolved through payment, settlement, or bankruptcy.

Does Tennessee have medical debt protections?

Protections vary. Check with your hospital about financial assistance programs. Nonprofit hospitals are required to have charity care policies under federal law.
