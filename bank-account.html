Bank Accounts and Bankruptcy in Tennessee

Understanding how bankruptcy interacts with your bank accounts in Tennessee can help you protect your money and avoid unexpected freezes or seizures.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Bank Account Levies and the Automatic Stay

Before bankruptcy, judgment creditors can obtain a court order to levy (freeze and seize) your bank account in Tennessee. The moment you file bankruptcy, the automatic stay prohibits all new levies.

  • New levies: Completely blocked by the stay
  • Pending levies: May need a motion to release frozen funds
  • Government debts: Tax levies are paused but not permanently blocked by the stay

Bank Setoff Rights

If you owe money to the same bank where your accounts are held (credit card, loan, line of credit), the bank has a right of setoff -- meaning they can take money from your deposit account to cover what you owe, even without a court order.

How to Protect Yourself

  • Before filing: Open a new account at a bank or credit union where you have no debts
  • Move direct deposit: Switch your payroll direct deposit to the new bank
  • Keep records: Do not move large sums that could look like a fraudulent transfer
  • Timing: Coordinate with your attorney to minimize risk around the filing date
Do not drain accounts or make large transfers without attorney guidance. Transfers of property before filing can be scrutinized as potential fraudulent conveyances.

Protecting Bank Deposits in Tennessee

In Chapter 7, the trustee can claim non-exempt cash in your bank accounts on the date of filing. In Tennessee:

  • Homestead exemption: $5,000 (individual) / $7,500 (joint) (does not protect cash)
  • Vehicle exemption: $10,000 (does not protect cash)
  • Federal exemptions: If you elect federal exemptions, the wildcard exemption can protect cash deposits

Many Tennessee filers protect bank deposits by timing the filing when account balances are low (right after paying normal living expenses like rent, utilities, and food).

Frequently Asked Questions

Can creditors freeze my bank account in Tennessee?

Yes. Before bankruptcy, a creditor with a judgment can levy (freeze and seize funds from) your bank account in Tennessee. After you file bankruptcy, the automatic stay immediately prohibits any new levies and may require release of frozen funds.

Should I open a new bank account before filing?

If you owe money to the same bank where you have checking or savings accounts, the bank may exercise its right of setoff -- taking money from your account to cover the debt. Opening an account at a bank where you have no debts before filing is a common protective measure.

Will bankruptcy affect my bank account?

Filing bankruptcy does not close your bank accounts. However, a Chapter 7 trustee could claim non-exempt funds. In Tennessee, check which exemptions protect your cash and bank deposits.
