Divorce and Bankruptcy in Tennessee

Divorce and bankruptcy often occur together. Understanding how they interact in Tennessee can help you protect your assets, manage shared debts, and plan the best filing strategy.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Filing Order: Bankruptcy Before, During, or After Divorce

1

Before Divorce

Filing a joint bankruptcy before divorce can eliminate shared unsecured debt and simplify the divorce property settlement. You save on filing fees by filing one joint case instead of two individual cases.

2

During Divorce

You can file bankruptcy during divorce proceedings. The automatic stay will pause any debt-related orders but does not affect custody, support, or the divorce itself. Coordination between courts is needed.

3

After Divorce

Filing after divorce means you file individually. Your divorce decree does not override bankruptcy law - a creditor can still come after you for joint debts even if the decree assigns them to your ex-spouse.

What Bankruptcy Cannot Discharge in Divorce

Under 11 U.S.C. section 523(a)(5) and (a)(15), the following divorce-related debts are not dischargeable:

  • Child support - all arrears and ongoing obligations
  • Alimony / spousal support - all arrears and ongoing obligations
  • Property settlement obligations - debts to a former spouse under a divorce decree, separation agreement, or property settlement (non-dischargeable in Chapter 7; potentially dischargeable in Chapter 13)
  • Hold-harmless agreements - if you agreed to pay a joint debt in the divorce, that obligation survives Chapter 7
Key difference: In Chapter 13, property division debts (section 523(a)(15)) may be dischargeable, while domestic support obligations (section 523(a)(5)) are never dischargeable.

Joint Debts and the Co-Debtor Stay

When one spouse files Chapter 13, the co-debtor stay under 11 U.S.C. section 1301 temporarily protects the non-filing spouse from creditors trying to collect on joint consumer debts. This protection does not exist in Chapter 7.

This is particularly important in Tennessee where both spouses may be on the mortgage, car loan, or credit cards. The co-debtor stay lasts as long as the Chapter 13 plan is active.

Tennessee Property Considerations

  • Homestead exemption: $5,000 (individual) / $7,500 (joint) - the bankruptcy and divorce courts both consider home equity
  • Vehicle exemption: $10,000
  • Federal exemptions available: Tennessee allows choosing federal exemptions, which may be better depending on your assets

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I file bankruptcy during a divorce in Tennessee?

Yes. You can file bankruptcy before, during, or after a divorce in Tennessee. The timing affects how debts and property are divided. Filing before the divorce can simplify property division by eliminating joint unsecured debt.

Does bankruptcy discharge alimony or child support?

No. Domestic support obligations including alimony and child support are not dischargeable in bankruptcy under any chapter. They are priority debts that must be paid in full.

What happens to joint debts in bankruptcy?

If one spouse files bankruptcy and discharges a joint debt, the other spouse remains fully liable. The creditor can pursue the non-filing spouse for the entire balance. A Chapter 13 co-debtor stay can temporarily protect the non-filing spouse during the repayment plan.
