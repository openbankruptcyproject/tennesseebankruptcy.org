Keep Your Car in Tennessee Bankruptcy

For most people, losing the car means losing the job. Here is how to keep your vehicle when filing bankruptcy in Tennessee.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Tennessee's Motor Vehicle Exemption: $0 (no specific motor vehicle exemption, but wildcard and federal exemptions may apply)

Tennessee lets you protect up to $0 (no specific motor vehicle exemption, but wildcard and federal exemptions may apply) of equity in a motor vehicle in bankruptcy. Equity is the fair market value of the car minus what you still owe on the loan. If your equity is below the exemption limit, the trustee cannot sell your car in Chapter 7.

Example: Your car is worth $12,000 and you owe $10,500 on the loan. Your equity is $1,500. If that sits below Tennessee's motor vehicle exemption, the car is protected. Keep making payments to the lender and the car stays with you through the case.

If your equity exceeds the exemption, you may still be able to keep the car by paying the trustee the non-exempt portion, using wildcard exemptions, or switching to Chapter 13.

Your Three Options in Chapter 7

Reaffirm

Sign a new agreement with the lender to keep the car and the loan. You remain personally liable after the case. Best if the car is worth close to the loan balance and you can afford the payment.

Surrender

Give the car back to the lender. Any deficiency balance is wiped out in the bankruptcy. Best if the car is deeply underwater or you cannot afford the payment.

Redeem

Pay the lender the car's fair market value in one lump sum (not the loan balance). Hard to do without outside financing, but can save thousands on underwater cars.

Chapter 13

Catch up missed payments over 3-5 years through the plan. May cramdown the loan balance to the car's value if you bought it more than 910 days ago (Chapter 13 details).

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep my car if I file bankruptcy in Tennessee?

Yes, in most cases. If your equity is below Tennessee's motor vehicle exemption ($0 (no specific motor vehicle exemption, but wildcard and federal exemptions may apply)) and you are current on payments, you can keep the car in Chapter 7. In Chapter 13, you can keep the car even if you are behind on payments by catching up through the plan.

What is reaffirmation?

A reaffirmation agreement is a contract with the lender to exclude the car loan from your bankruptcy discharge. You promise to keep paying and keep the car, but you also remain personally liable if you default later. Only sign one if you can afford the payment.

What is Chapter 13 cramdown?

Cramdown lets you reduce the loan balance to the car's actual value if you bought it more than 910 days (2.5 years) ago. This can save thousands on underwater vehicles. Talk to a bankruptcy attorney about whether cramdown fits your case.
