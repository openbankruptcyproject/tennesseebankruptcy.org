Keep Your House in Tennessee Bankruptcy

Your home is often your most important asset. Here is how Tennessee's homestead exemption and bankruptcy law work together to help you keep your house.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Tennessee's Homestead Exemption: $5,000

Tennessee's homestead exemption protects up to $5,000 of equity in your primary residence ($7,500 for married couples filing jointly). If your equity is below this amount, your home is fully protected in Chapter 7.

To calculate your equity, take the fair market value of your home and subtract all mortgages and liens. The result is your equity. If that number fits within the exemption limit, the trustee cannot sell your home.

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13 for Your Home

Chapter 7

  • Homestead exemption protects equity up to $5,000
  • Eliminates unsecured debt, making mortgage payments more affordable
  • Does NOT stop foreclosure long-term - only delays it during the case
  • You must stay current on mortgage payments to keep the home
  • Best if you are current on mortgage and need to eliminate other debts

Chapter 13

  • Stops foreclosure through the automatic stay
  • Catch up on missed payments over 3-5 years
  • May strip off underwater second mortgages (lien stripping)
  • Protects home regardless of equity amount
  • Best if you are behind on mortgage payments

Foreclosure in Tennessee

Tennessee primarily uses non-judicial foreclosure. The process typically takes about 2-3 months from start to sale, and Tennessee law provides a statutory right of redemption of two years, unless waived in the deed of trust (most Tennessee deeds waive this).

If you are facing foreclosure in Tennessee, bankruptcy can stop the process. The automatic stay halts all foreclosure proceedings the moment you file. Chapter 13 then gives you a structured way to catch up on missed payments while keeping your home.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep my house if I file bankruptcy in Tennessee?

In most cases, yes. Tennessee's homestead exemption protects up to $5,000 of equity in your home. If your equity is below that amount, your home is fully protected in Chapter 7. In Chapter 13, you keep your home while catching up on missed mortgage payments through a 3-5 year repayment plan.

Can Chapter 13 stop foreclosure in Tennessee?

Yes. Filing Chapter 13 triggers the automatic stay, which immediately stops foreclosure proceedings. You can then catch up on missed mortgage payments over 3-5 years through your repayment plan while continuing to make regular monthly payments going forward.

What happens to my second mortgage in bankruptcy?

If your home is worth less than what you owe on the first mortgage, Chapter 13 may allow you to strip off (remove) the second mortgage entirely. The second mortgage is treated as unsecured debt. This is called lien stripping and can save homeowners thousands.
