Chapter 13 Bankruptcy Eligibility in Tennessee

Chapter 13 lets Tennessee residents keep their property while repaying debts over 3-5 years. Here are the eligibility requirements.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Core Eligibility Requirements

1. Regular Income

You must have regular income to fund a Chapter 13 repayment plan. Acceptable sources include:

  • Wages or salary
  • Self-employment income
  • Social Security benefits
  • Pension or retirement income
  • Rental income
  • Regular contributions from a spouse or household member

2. Debt Limits

Your total debts (secured plus unsecured) must not exceed $2,750,000. This combined limit was established by the Bankruptcy Threshold Adjustment and Technical Corrections Act of 2022.

3. Tax Filing

You must have filed all required federal and state tax returns for the four years before filing.

4. Credit Counseling

You must complete a credit counseling course from an approved provider within 180 days before filing.

Plan Length: 3 Years vs. 5 Years

Below Median Income

If your household income is at or below the Tennessee median, your plan can be as short as 36 months (3 years) and no longer than 60 months (5 years).

Above Median Income

If your household income exceeds the Tennessee median, your plan must be 60 months (5 years). This is called the applicable commitment period.

Prior Filing Bars for Chapter 13

Under 11 U.S.C. section 1328(f), you cannot receive a Chapter 13 discharge if:

  • You received a Chapter 7 discharge within the past 4 years
  • You received a Chapter 13 discharge within the past 2 years

These time bars only prevent a discharge - you can still file Chapter 13 to use the automatic stay and structured repayment even without eligibility for a discharge.

Chapter 13 Advantages in Tennessee

  • Keep your home: Catch up on mortgage arrears while protecting up to $5,000 (individual) / $7,500 (joint) in equity
  • Keep your car: Restructure auto loans (potential cramdown if older than 910 days) with $10,000 exemption protection
  • No means test barrier: Unlike Chapter 7, high earners can file Chapter 13
  • Co-debtor stay: Protects co-signers on consumer debts during the plan
  • Broader discharge: Some debts not dischargeable in Chapter 7 may be discharged in Chapter 13

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the debt limits for Chapter 13 in Tennessee?

As of 2024, the debt limit for Chapter 13 is $2,750,000 in combined secured and unsecured debts. This limit was updated by the Bankruptcy Threshold Adjustment and Technical Corrections Act. If your debts exceed this amount, you may need to file Chapter 11 instead.

Do I need regular income to file Chapter 13?

Yes. Chapter 13 requires regular income sufficient to fund a repayment plan. This can include wages, salary, commissions, self-employment income, Social Security, pension, rental income, or even consistent support from a spouse or domestic partner.

How long is a Chapter 13 plan in Tennessee?

If your income is below the Tennessee median for your household size, your plan can be 3-5 years. If your income is above the median, your plan must be 5 years. The plan cannot exceed 5 years (60 months).
